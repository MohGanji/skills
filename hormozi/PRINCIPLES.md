# The 13 Principles -- Deep Treatment

Hormozi's internal checklist for any piece of advertising or copy. Each is a "best converting nugget," not a textbook. Use the examples as intuition pumps for spotting what to improve.

---

## 1. Headlines come first

The headline is the first and most important thing. Per David Ogilvy: once you've written the headline, you've spent 80 cents of your advertising dollar. Most people will only ever see the headline.

- **Law of small numbers**: moving a 1% click-through to 3% triples how many people see everything else. Nothing downstream in the funnel triples results -- a 20-50% lift is already a monster split test. A great headline can beat the control 2-3x, sometimes 5x.
- The best advertisers obsess over the hook. A winning hook gets used to death while you try to beat it.
- **Curiosity is king. Different is ideal. Sexy works.** Never run an ad without a headline -- all ads have headlines, there are just good ones and bad ones. A missing headline is a bad headline.
- The headline is different wrapping paper for the same offer. It should **grab the reader by the throat**.
- **Find winners ahead of time**: study the first 3 seconds of organic content (yours and others') -- visually and verbally, including the background. That's where the working hooks are.
- **Steal like an artist**: pull hooks from another industry (travel, weight loss) into yours.
- Favorite template: **"How to [yay / good thing] without [boo / bad thing], even if [biggest fear/insecurity]."** Example: "How to speak confidently in front of strangers without ever practicing, even if you're deathly afraid of public speaking."

---

## 2. Say what only you can say

If you've done something remarkable or different, say it. **Proof will always outdo promise.**

- Risk real money / earn real results, then claim them. (Hormozi opens by noting he spends tens of millions on ads in companies *he owns* -- not as an agency taking credit for clients.)
- Be the only one -- and you get to choose the pond you're one-of-a-kind in. "The only triple-black-belt in the area." "The only person who's helped vegan powerlifter moms over 35."
- **Competitors can copy your offer; they cannot copy your proof.** Proof must be earned.
- Story is a moat. No AI avatar can have *done* something in the real world. "Why should I listen to you?" stays at the top of every prospect's mind, and as trust gets scarcer, verification matters more.

---

## 3. Call out who you're looking for (and who you're not)

Calling out who you're *not* for is even more powerful than calling out who you are for.

- "If you're below $250k/year, this isn't for you -- go consume my free content." That positions you sharply in the market.
- **Saying no polarizes the right customers toward you.** Vanilla ("everything to all people") means nobody feels pulled in. You only need a few hundred right customers; there are billions of people.
- You never advertise to the masses -- you advertise to **one person**. The goal: the reader self-selects and thinks "this person is talking to *me* -- how do they know my secrets?"
- **Levels of awareness** (hook each differently):
  - **Unaware** -- hook with pure curiosity.
  - **Problem aware** -- "Do you get up to pee more than twice a night?"
  - **Solution aware** -- "Ever tried collection software that didn't work as promised?"
  - **Product aware** -- "Ever tried *this* type of creatine vs. that one?"
  - **Most aware** -- already knows you and the product; just make offers.
- **Specificity creates compelling copy.** Everyone is selfish and thinks they're the center of the universe -- speak to one person as if they're alone in it.
- Avoid marketing-ease. Don't say "Are you overweight?" Break the word into a **moment**: "Do you have trouble getting upstairs, or going for a walk without a rash forming between your legs and sweating in places you didn't know you could?"
- **Name the moment.** Bar-consulting example: not generic copy, but "When you look behind the bar, does it look like a bomb went off?" The prospect lived that moment in the last seven days -- fresh pain lowers the threshold to act. Ask: what are the moments someone experiences fatness, brokenness, frustration, loneliness? The moments are what they remember; vague words never make anyone emote.

---

## 4. Always have a reason why

It barely matters what the reason is -- what matters is that you *have* one.

- Cialdini's copier study: "Can I cut in line *because* I need to make copies" got yes -- but so did "because my cat is back home." The word **"because"** is one of the most influential in English. Any reason beats no reason.
- The **fraternity party planner** has a very low bar for a reason: "Timmy got his wisdom teeth out -- kegger." "It's Tuesday -- let's drink." Your dad's birthday, your dog's birthday, the business anniversary -- four dates is a month of promotions.
- Bonus points if the reason ties to **urgency or scarcity**.
- Give the reason to take **the next single step**. Every line of copy sells the next line; the copy sells the click; the click sells the opt-in; the opt-in sells showing up. **A confused mind doesn't buy -- and certainly doesn't click.** Sell one step at a time.

---

## 5. Damaging admissions

In Hormozi's experience, nothing is more powerful. Owning a bias or flaw upfront makes people believe you more, because you've been truthful.

- **What you sacrifice in promise, you gain in trust -- and trust trumps promise.** (A referral needs almost no promise because trust is borrowed from the introduction.)
- The **Eminem** move (8 Mile): own all your flaws first -- "I'm short, I'm white..." -- and the opponent has nothing left to attack with.
- **The "but" mechanic**: "[true, believable, damaging statement] **but** [the thing you want believed]." The part after "but" is what gets emphasized.
  - "These markers smell like crap, **but** they write 4x longer and don't dry out." → you believe the longevity claim more.
  - Flip it -- "They write 4x longer **but** they smell like crap" -- and you've emphasized the smell instead. **Order matters.**
  - "Viagra only works 4-6 hours, **but** you'll have the time of your life." "Our websites are ugly as hell, **but** they convert." "Our systems are incredibly boring, **but** they work."
- Exercise: write down every flaw, every complaint, everything you could do better -- and **claim them before the prospect thinks them.** Telling the truth makes you authentic in a crowded market. Humor on top is bonus points: "Our gym has terrible parking, the AC is on and off, the equipment isn't the best -- but our sessions are more fun than anyone else's, because it's all we've got."

---

## 6. Show, don't tell

Not just "use images" -- **break down the reasoning into the experience**.

- Don't say "get more sales." Describe the moment: *running out of order forms because customers are reading their credit cards to you over the phone too fast.* That is the experience of more sales.
- "Get more leads" → the overwhelm of too many phones ringing and not enough salespeople. A gym owner wants to *see the gym full*, the line around the corner, having to rearrange the floor to fit more people in a session.
- **Future problems they'd prefer over their current ones** are among the most compelling things in copy. Describing problems they haven't thought of yet signals you've actually been there.
- In creative, demonstrate it; in copy, use **descriptive, specific language about the moment** the outcome becomes real: "What moment will they realize this happened? What does it look and feel like?"
- Bonus: the way *you* describe that moment is unique to you, so your copy always sounds fresh and different from everyone using the same words.

---

## 7. Tie benefits to status

Go past "benefits not features" -- tie the benefit to **status**.

- "A cookbook so fast and easy that all your friends will wonder how you found the time to be fit and cook for your family." The friends awarding status is the extra level.
- **What / Who / When framework** (Leads book, p.140): identify *who* awards the status -- family, friends, colleagues, rivals, competitors -- "who are they vanquishing?" Humans are competitive; status raises odds of resources and mating.
- Status is **subgroup-specific**: for a mother it's other parents wondering how her kids excel; for a 20-year-old hustle-bro it might be a Lamborghini. Meet them where they are. (Hormozi himself prefers "Mozy-mobiles" -- $7k cash cars -- rich in the bank, not in others' eyes -- but the copy must match the reader.)
- **Past / present / future** × multiple status-givers = endless angles. "So your friends' mouths will drop with envy."

---

## 8. Urgency and scarcity

Locked together in people's minds, but different:

- **Scarcity = quantity.** Only 100 widgets / 100 spots. Once gone, no one can buy -- whether it sold out in 5 seconds or a year.
- **Urgency = time.** Unlimited supply, but the promotion ends at midnight.
- You can use both. The goal: show others want it and they must act fast -- lowering the action threshold.
- **The only secret is making it legitimate.** (Offers book: 4 types of urgency p.112, scarcity p.104.) When someone wants to buy after midnight, or after the 100 spots are gone, **you say no** -- to protect the reputation. Then next time, the first buyer is the one who missed out. **Dig your well before you're thirsty**; underselling demand means you always have more later. Fake scarcity that never sells out loses all its power.

---

## 9. Implied authority

People trust authority figures more -- so imply it (legitimately). This is where **proof beats promise** on *believability*: the prospect must want the thing first, then their "why should I believe this?" alarms fire.

- Restate the "only one" claims from #1/#2: "the only double-secret black belt in the area," "transformed over a thousand people to black belt," a pro-athlete client who lets you say so.
- **Longevity**: "in business 40 years" signals legitimacy. Or aggregate: "in aggregate we've done over 20 years of advertising" (four people × five years).
- **Tiny awards matter**: "as seen on Oprah," "best boot camp in Vegas." Even an obscure list lets you open with "Did you find us on the Vegas best boot camp list?"
- **Social proof**: 4.6 stars with 4,000 reviews beats 5.0 with 3 reviews every time -- volume implies they've been around and know what they're doing. Document and substantiate everything you've done and everyone you've helped.

## 10. Always have a PS

The **PS is a "power sentence"** -- the second-most-read part of any ad after the headline. (The PPS is third.) If it's that widely read, make it work.

Three go-to strategies:
1. **Reward the reader** (especially in emails): jokes, fun, light stuff -- so they're trained to read and click the PS. Then when you drop a strong CTA there, they click.
2. **Disclaimer of who it's *not* for**: "By the way, if you're just getting started, this isn't for you -- this is only for people doing X, Y, Z. Click here." Works really well.
3. **One-sentence recap** of the whole pitch: "We've helped a thousand people, we're good at making black belts, if you're serious click here." For the skimmers who jump to the bottom.

Add a **PPS** for a second point if needed -- it's still the third-most-read line.

## 11. Clear CTA

Amazingly effective, dead simple -- the only thing simpler is not doing it. If you don't tell someone exactly what to do next, fewer people do it.

- Make it **stupid-simple**: "Click the button, on the next page fill in your info, then pick a convenient time to come in so you won't miss out." State **what happens next.**
- **Say-do correspondence**: when you tell someone what will happen and it does, you gain influence. That's the **Martha Stewart** mechanic -- follow these 18 steps → you get a cake → it tastes good → status from family → she gains influence, so you follow her next instruction. **Make promises, keep promises -- repeatedly, where they take action and your promise comes true.** Influence compounds only when they *act* and the promise is fulfilled.
- Hormozi named his company **acquisition.com** so the name itself is the CTA.

---

## 12. Third-grade reading level

Most copy doesn't convert because people pause to understand it -- and the moment they pause, they stop. Assume the prospect is rushed, distracted, selfish, and reading below a third-grade level.

- **Fancy = friction. Simple = sales.** Write at ninth-grade level and you auto-disqualify two-thirds of readers who can't read at that level. The most popular books in America are deliberately written simply.
- Run every ad through a free **reading-level tool** and keep editing down to third grade. Great copy makes the words fade into the background -- the reader just *understands*.
- Quick tips:
  - **Short sentences. Small words. Big promises. Big proof.**
  - **Few adverbs, better verbs**: "he sprinted," not "he quickly ran."
  - **Word concision** -- fewest words to make the point -- *but* keep each word simple.
  - **Simplicity trumps concision.** ("Concision" itself is a ninth-grade word.) If the shorter word is the harder word, choose the simpler one.

---

## 13. Use humor (bonus)

Whenever possible, add humor -- the "extra sauce."

- The **Old Spice** campaign took a non-leader to market dominator, and it was funny.
- **Entertain *and* educate**: entertainment goes broad (reach), education goes deep (changes behavior → the click). You need both, but the education is what moves the action.
- **First to cut, last to require**: you can build converting ads with the other 12 alone. Humor is the sizzle on top.
- The hard part is being funny for everyone -- so **test like a comedian**: comedians run material across many clubs, keep the ~8 minutes that land, frontload them, and build a 60-minute special from tested two-minute additions across cities. "Converts" in comedy = laughs; in ads = the desired action.
